Discrete-Time¶

A discrete-time Markov chain is sequence of random variables

\[(X_t)_{t \geq 0}.\]

Possible values of \(X_i\) form a finite or countable state space \(S\).

Time Homogenous Processes¶

Time-homogeneous Markov chains (or stationary Markov chains) are processes where

\[\Pr(X_{n+1}=x\mid X_{n}=y)=\Pr(X_{n}=x\mid X_{n-1}=y)\,\]

for all \(n\). The probability of the transition is independent of \(n\).

Markov Property¶

\[\begin{split}\Pr(X_{n+1}=x\mid X_{1}=x_{1},X_{2}=x_{2},\ldots ,X_{n}=x_{n})= \\ \Pr(X_{n+1}=x\mid X_{n}=x_{n})\end{split}\]

Transition Matrix¶

For \(i, j \in S\)

\[\begin{split}P(i, j) &\geq 0 \\ \sum_{j \in S} P(i, j) &= 1\end{split}\]

Function definition

\[P : S \times S \to [0, 1]\]

Marginal Distribution¶

\[\boldsymbol{\mu}_{n}(j) = \sum_{i \in S} \boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}(i) P^{n}(i, j)\]

In vector form

\[\boldsymbol{\mu}_n = \boldsymbol{\mu}_0 P^{n}\]

Transition Probability¶

Probability of going from state \(i\) to \(j\) in \(n\) timesteps

\[\Pr(X_{n}=j\mid X_{0}=i) = P^{n}(i, j)\]

Expected Number of Visits¶

\[M_t(i, j) = \sum_{s = 0}^{t} P^{s}(i, j)\]

Limiting Distribution¶

Also stationary distribution for finite Markov chains.

\[\boldsymbol{\pi}(j) = \sum_{i \in S} \boldsymbol{\pi}(i) P(i, j)\]

In vector form

\[\boldsymbol{\pi} = \boldsymbol{\pi} P\]

and

\[\sum_{i \in S} \boldsymbol{\pi}(i) = 1\]

Expected Number of Hits¶

\[g_n(i) = \sum_{j \in S} M_{n} (i, j) c(j)\]

In vector form

\[g_{n} = M_{n} c\]

where \(c(i)\) is the cost of hitting node \(i\)

Expected number of hits a the long run

\[g(i) = \sum_{j \in S} \boldsymbol{\pi}(j) c(j)\]

Travel Time¶

To group \(A\)

\[T_A = \min\{t \geq 0 : X_{t} \in A\}\]

Collection of expected travel times

\[\begin{split}\begin{cases} f(i) = 1 + \sum_{y \notin A} P(i, j) f(j) & i \notin A \\ f(i) = 0 & i \in A \end{cases}\end{split}\]

smallest non-negative solution.

Hitting Probability¶

Group \(A\)

\[\begin{split}\begin{cases} f(i) = \sum_{y \notin A} P(i, j) f(j) & i \notin A \\ f(i) = 1 & i \in A \end{cases}\end{split}\]

smallest non-negative solution.

Reversibility¶

A Markov chain is said to be reversible if there is a probability distribution \(\boldsymbol{\pi}\) over its states such that

\[\boldsymbol{\pi}(i) P(i, j) = \boldsymbol{\pi}(j)P(j, i), \quad \forall x, y \in S\]
